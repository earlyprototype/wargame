## Module 1: Why Wargame? History and Purposes (00:00 - 11:00)

### Core Concepts (M1)

- Professional wargaming as a structured simulation to develop decision-making experience and decision-making information
- Safe-to-fail, low-cost experimentation with real human adversaries and chance
- Historical lineage from Prussian Kriegsspiel (von Reisswitz) to modern doctrine
- Complementarity with live exercises and analytical models; each has distinct strengths/limits

### Main Content (M1)

- Wargames provide officers a realistic but risk-contained environment to practise decisions and explore concepts that would be impossible or costly to test live. They prioritise plausibility over prediction, surfacing what could happen and how leaders might respond.
- Two primary objectives recur: training (rehearsal, judgment under uncertainty) and discovery (testing ideas, surfacing vulnerabilities, informing doctrine and procurement).
- The practice is longstanding. From 1820s Prussia through interwar Germany and Allied navies in WWII, militaries have used games to compress time, iterate tactics, and compare courses of action.
- Wargames are most useful when triangulated with field exercises and quantitative models; each method checks the others’ blind spots.

### Key Examples (M1)

1. 1824 demonstration of Kriegsspiel to the Prussian General Staff: “This is not a game, this is training for war” (≈ 00:05:02–00:05:23)
2. 1927 German wargame cohort including future senior commanders (Guderian, von Manstein, Dönitz, etc.) (≈ 00:05:33–00:06:03)
3. Royal Navy WATU (1942–45): tabletop games revealed U-boat “inside the convoy” tactics and informed counter‑tactics (≈ 00:08:41–00:09:58)

### Common Misconceptions (M1)

- “They’re just board games.” In fact they are designed analytical tools with adjudication, data, and defined learning aims.
- “Wargames predict outcomes.” They explore plausible behaviours and decision pathways; they are not oracles.
- “One method suffices.” Best practice blends games, live trials, and modelling to reduce individual method bias.

## Module 2: Humans vs Models — Why Boards Still Matter (11:00 - 16:00)

### Core Concepts (M2)

- Human creativity, deception, and adaptation often outperform scripted or non‑cheating AI
- Models and synthetic environments are powerful but assumption‑sensitive
- Board wargames retain value for adversarial reasoning, friction, and chance
- Games are vulnerable to incentives and poor design; governance matters

### Main Content (M2)

- Computers can simulate at scale, but adversarial human ingenuity and competitive pressure in games often expose edge cases models miss. Chance and fog-of-war inject realism into decision-making under uncertainty.
- Synthetic environments and statistical models are indispensable, yet their outputs reflect embedded assumptions. When those are wrong, confidence can be misleading.
- Tabletop systems force transparent trade‑offs and sequencing, making interdependencies (e.g., ISR → targeting → fires) visible to participants.

### Key Examples (M2)

1. Gulf War planning: high‑casualty predictions in some computer models vs a modified commercial wargame (“Gulf Strike”) indicating rapid victory with minimal losses (≈ 00:11:36–00:12:16)
2. Nimitz’s post‑war observation: wartime events largely mirrored US pre‑war games; little was a surprise except kamikaze tactics (≈ 00:10:48–00:11:00)

### Common Misconceptions (M2)

- “More tech = more accuracy.” Fidelity without correct assumptions can increase error confidence.
- “AI opponents are good proxies for humans.” Unless allowed to cheat, many AIs lack human creativity.
- “Dice make games unrealistic.” Chance models battlefield stochasticity and forces robust plans.

## Module 3: From Joint Ops to MDO and CDC (16:00 - 19:30)

### Core Concepts (M3)

- Joint operations vs multi‑domain operations (MDO): adding space, cyber, and wider state power
- Cross‑Domain Command (CDC): orchestration and synchronisation across domains/actors, not micro‑control
- Trust, decentralisation, and horizontal coordination between multi‑domain teams
- Leveraging civilian capabilities and AI assistants within command processes

### Main Content (M3)

- MDO extends joint operations by integrating space, cyber, economic and informational levers with land, maritime, and air. Success depends on synchronising effects across domains and state instruments.
- The CDC concept tested here created four multi‑domain teams under a light‑touch higher HQ. Teams shared assets, self‑coordinated, and could reorganise to pursue common strategic aims.
- A “grey cell” represented civilian capabilities (ministries, satellite providers, big tech) that Blue could task for ISR, resilience, and information operations. Rules were adjusted mid‑event due to its outsized impact.
- An AI assistant (H‑Cal) supported status queries and intel summarisation to reduce cognitive load and time costs during timed turns.

### Key Examples (M3)

1. Four multi‑domain teams with a small HQ emphasising mission‑type command and lateral coordination (≈ 00:16:12–00:17:11)
2. Grey cell: public hotline for geo‑tagged reporting; commercial satellite imagery; tech firm cyber support (rules later tuned) (≈ 00:17:07–00:18:08)
3. H‑Cal AI: rapid search and intel summarisation to aid tempo (≈ 00:18:28–00:18:52)

### Common Misconceptions (M3)

- “MDO is just joint ops with extra steps.” CDC changes who coordinates what, when, and how, including non‑military levers.
- “Cross‑domain command centralises control.” The experiment emphasised decentralised coordination and trust between teams.
- “Civilian tools are peripheral.” Civil capabilities can create asymmetric advantages and resilience.

## Module 4: Scenario, Map, Forces, Objectives (19:30 - 26:30)

### Core Concepts (M4)

- Fictional theatre: Blue = Otso; Red = Bothnia (west) and De Novia (east); Nastria = neutral
- Action economy: timed turns, action points, persistent capabilities, and domain “cards”
- Objectives and influence: defend/restore vs seize/cut‑off; propaganda/information meter (+10 to −10)
- Force quality asymmetry: Blue’s NATO‑grade kit vs Red’s largely Soviet/Russian with some Chinese systems

### Main Content (M4)

- Map: Otso central, flanked by Bothnia and De Novia, with key resource fields, pipelines, and two seas. Red had forces massed at borders; some disputed hexes already occupied.
- Blue (Otso ~12.5m) fielded high‑end systems (F‑35, F‑22, Patriot, THAAD, Virginia‑class SSN). Red (De Novia ~75m; Bothnia ~52m) leaned on legacy Soviet/Russian equipment plus limited newer Chinese designs.
- Blue was split into four multi‑domain teams plus a light HQ; Red used a traditional command structure with one commander synchronising four geographic sectors.
- Objectives: Blue to defend urban centres and infrastructure, hold resources, restore borders, counter hybrid pressure, and establish a blockade. Red to breach/prevent blockade, seize resources, cut Otso off from the southern sea, and sway Nastria.
- Information environment tracked via a bidirectional meter influencing domestic and international support throughout the exercise.

### Key Examples (M4)

1. Blue objectives and constraints (force preservation, civilian impact minimisation) (≈ 00:28:10–00:28:27 context introduced earlier at ≈ 00:19:46–00:26:22)
2. Red aims: seize resource fields; cut sea access; keep or flip Nastria (≈ 00:26:08–00:26:22)
3. Influence meter: +10 Blue to −10 Red to represent information dominance vs collapse (≈ 00:26:26–00:26:40)

### Common Misconceptions (M4)

- “Superior kit guarantees success.” Mass, initiative, sequencing, and political/information factors remain decisive.
- “Civilian tech is optional.” The grey cell materially shifted options, tempo, and resilience.
- “Blockade is purely naval.” Success required multi‑domain coordination and political shaping.

## Module 5: Pre-kinetic Setup, ISR, Tasking, Clarifications (26:30 - 34:00)

### Core Concepts (M5)

- Pre-kinetic phase: manoeuvre, ISR and jamming allowed; no kinetic fires
- Establishing persistent capabilities to generate repeatable per-turn effects
- Interpreting guidance and ROE: forward defence vs elastic defence
- Action economy trade-offs before the first shots

### Main Content (M5)

- Both sides manoeuvred ISR assets (satellites, aircraft, drones). Jamming was permitted; shoot-downs were not. This produced frustration (e.g., lumbering MPA roaming international airspace unmolested) but forced focus on posture and set-up.
- Teams invested actions to create persistent capabilities (use-once-to-establish, then once-per-turn effects if not destroyed), shaping later tempo.
- Blue, starting from peacetime positions, made larger redeployments and gained relatively more reconnaissance than Red.
- A doctrinal debate emerged: hold the border strictly vs trade space for time. Higher HQ clarified Blue could yield terrain temporarily where sensible but must prevent Red from bringing urban centres into range.

### Key Examples (M5)

1. Maritime patrol aircraft collecting ISR without kinetic risk (≈ 00:27:12–00:27:22)
2. Border posture debate: forward line vs high‑ground defence-in-depth (≈ 00:28:41–00:30:01)
3. HQ guidance: temporary withdrawal acceptable; protect cities (≈ 00:30:10–00:30:19)

### Common Misconceptions (M5)

- “Holding the border” always means forward defence. Elastic defence can better preserve forces and civilians.
- ISR immunity equals irrelevance of EW. Jamming constrained ISR value even without kinetic counters.
- Pre-kinetic turns are idle time. They are decisive for setting persistent effects and posture.

## Module 6: Kinetic Opening, Attrition, Naval Sub-plot (34:00 - 45:00)

### Core Concepts (M6)

- Red’s reconnaissance-by-fire and massed assaults vs Blue’s absorb-and‑attrit plan
- Ammunition consumption reality under pressure; action point and ammo economy
- Counter-battery priority on Red MRLs/SPGs; naval long-range fires as land enablers
- Subsurface asymmetry and dice friction; AP efficiency of platforms

### Main Content (M6)

- Once authorised to cross, Red pushed large stacks into Blue’s defences with limited recon, forcing Blue to reveal positions. Early combats saw Red “mag-dump” ammunition on sacrificial vanguards to maximise initial effects.
- Blue prioritised force protection, camouflage/re‑conceal, and high‑value fires (e.g., HIMARS vs heavy MRLs). Ammunition conservation intent quickly gave way to practical escalation under fire.
- At sea, Blue’s Virginia‑class SSN reliably attrited Red surface combatants (often a cruiser per turn), buying Blue naval freedom. Red Kilo boats, though theoretically dangerous, suffered poor detection/attack rolls and were hunted down.
- In the air, B‑2 strikes routinely penetrated Red IADS; surface combatants expended VLS heavily for land‑attack and intercepts in key turns.

### Key Examples (M6)

1. First destroyed unit belonged to Blue despite Red’s quality gap (≈ 00:31:46–00:31:55 context from early kinetic)
2. Arleigh Burke nearly emptied land‑attack loadout in one turn; heavy interceptor expenditure on another (≈ 00:33:14–00:33:25)
3. Virginia SSN pattern: appear → sink a cruiser → vanish (≈ 00:32:24–00:32:31)
4. B‑2 strike packages bypassing IADS for repeat effects (≈ 00:36:55–00:37:11)

### Common Misconceptions (M6)

- “You can ration munitions strictly under fire.” Combat pressure often forces early expenditure to prevent greater losses.
- “Submarines guarantee naval dominance.” ASW posture, detection, and chance materially mediate effects.
- “Blockade progress is independent from land fight.” Naval contributions to land fires delayed blockade establishment but shaped decisive ground outcomes.

## Module 7: Cyber/EW Sequencing, Info War, Space Gambit (45:00 - 58:30)

### Core Concepts (M7)

- Sequenced, composite cyber/EW for theatre-wide C2 disruption
- Resource pooling and trust: trading action points to centralise cyber effects
- Information environment as an operational line of effort
- Space as a contested/deniable domain; resilience without space enablers

### Main Content (M7)

- Blue concentrated cyber/EW at theatre level. “B‑SOD 1” underperformed; “B‑SOD 2” achieved major tactical network degradation for Red: up to −7 to long‑range fires and air‑defence intercepts, later repaired to −4.
- Effect illustration: with EmRIC defending, Red Uragan’s per‑shot hit chance fell from ~17.5% to ~11% (−4) and ~5% (−7), while Blue’s incoming munitions faced weakened Red intercepts. Rules arguably favoured high‑end systems under flat penalties, raising realism questions.
- Information fight: Red’s early momentum dropped the meter to −4 through territorial gains, casualties, and deepfakes; Blue’s later battlefield success and humanitarian pivot swung sentiment back, with Nastria becoming “neutral” but supportive via shipping.
- Space gambit: Red attempted Kessler syndrome to deny space to both sides and collapse C2; a poor roll failed to start the debris cascade, missing a potentially transformative scenario shift.

### Key Examples (M7)

1. −7/−4 cyber modifiers hampering Red fires and IADS (≈ 00:43:21–00:43:36)
2. Uragan vs EmRIC probability swing under modifiers (≈ 00:42:38–00:43:17)
3. Red info push to −4 on the influence meter; potential −7/−8 (≈ 00:36:12–00:36:31)
4. Kessler attempt fails due to dice, preventing space denial (≈ 00:46:45–00:46:55)

### Common Misconceptions (M7)

- “Cyber alone stops armour immediately.” It requires preparation, sequencing, and combination with kinetic/EW to be decisive.
- “Advanced systems always dominate under cyber/EW stress.” Some low‑tech methods degrade less; model penalties should reflect that nuance.
- “Space services are assured.” Plans must be robust to partial or total denial of space‑based enablers.

## Module 8: Outcomes, Lessons, Design Tweaks, Next Steps (58:30 - 01:04:33)

### Core Concepts (M8)

- CDC feasibility: decentralised teams coordinated effectively with minimal HQ friction
- Importance of scarcity and pressure for testing trust, prioritisation, and adaptation
- Scenario design levers: fog‑of‑war transparency, UAS density, cyber opacity, civilian tech access, strategic layer, curveballs
- Balance of quality vs mass, initiative, and information effects

### Main Content (M8)

- Teams integrated domains early and often; resource requests were disciplined, with combat arms recognising cyber/EW efficiency at theatre scale.
- Scenario balance favoured Blue (initiative, quality kit, grey‑cell access). Red lacked infantry mass to screen abundant artillery, opening exploitable vulnerabilities.
- Design improvements suggested: reduce fog‑of‑war near front; proliferate small UAS; model counter‑air via ground networks; add pre‑war strategic phase for cyber/info shaping; give Red meaningful civilian tech channels; obscure cyber effects to require trust; consider forcing a space‑denied turn to observe CDC resilience.
- Harder scenarios likely accelerate reorganisation and innovation, better testing CDC under stress and scarcity.

### Key Examples (M8)

1. Blue’s action economy multipliers (grey cell, powerful air/sea cards) vs Red’s mass but thin infantry (≈ 00:55:57–00:56:20)
2. B‑2 vs Tu‑160 effectiveness and survivability gap (≈ 00:58:32–00:58:54)
3. Proposed umpire “curveballs” (e.g., enforce space denial to collect behavioural data) (≈ 01:01:50–01:02:06)

### Common Misconceptions (M8)

- “Winning confirms the concept.” True validation requires testing under adverse, stressed conditions.
- “Quality eclipses quantity.” Mass, initiative, and info effects can offset quality gaps; balance matters.
- “Cyber value is self‑evident.” Without transparent cards, buy‑in must be built via trust and demonstrated impact.

## Key Formulas/Processes

```text
Process: Theatre-wide Cyber/EW Disruption (CDC context)
1) Reconnaissance: Map adversary C2 pathways; collect exploits (incl. civilian partners)
2) Preparation: Establish persistent EW; stage ISR for BDA and re‑targets
3) Opening Move: Execute exploit(s) that boost success odds for follow‑on actions
4) Main Effort: Concurrent offensive cyber packages targeting tactical network nodes
5) EW Overlay: Jam GNSS/comms to compound disruption; protect own C2 (EMCON where needed)
6) Kinetic Exploitation: Time long‑range fires/air ops during peak disruption window
7) Sustain/Repair: Reserve actions for counter‑countermeasures; re‑attack if needed
8) Assess: BDA, info-meter effects, and friendly logistics impacts; adjust sequencing
```

## Appendix: CLI Roleplay Wargame Design Notes (for LLM-driven play)

### Game Loop (Single Turn)

1. Admin/UMPIRE reveals public state snapshot; secret states remain hidden.
2. Each SIDE allocates Action Points (AP) to: Move, ISR, Cyber/EW, Fires, Maritime, Air, Civil (Grey), Info Ops, Logistics.
3. Resolve sequencing: ISR → Cyber/EW → Air/Maritime → Fires → Ground → Info Ops → Logistics/Repair.
4. Apply chance/adjudication; record effects; update visibility and influence meter.
5. Check victory triggers; proceed to next turn.

### Roles and Responsibilities

- BLUE TEAM: Four multi-domain task groups; coordinate laterally; request theatre cyber/EW.
- RED TEAM: Theatre commander with geographic sub-commands; mass, probe, exploit.
- GREY CELL: Civilian providers (SAT imagery, comms, tech support, humanitarian) with constraints.
- UMPIRE/ADMIN: Enforces rules, fog‑of‑war, dice, random events; manages secret state.

### Core JSON State Model (minimal)

```json
{
  "turn": 1,
  "influence": 0,  // -10 (Red) to +10 (Blue)
  "spaceDenied": false,
  "sides": {
    "BLUE": {
      "ap": 8,
      "units": [{"id":"B-2","type":"air","pos":"A3","ammo":6,"concealed":true}],
      "persistent": {"ewJammer": true, "satISR": true},
      "netPenalty": 0
    },
    "RED": {
      "ap": 8,
      "units": [{"id":"Uragan-1","type":"mrl","pos":"D5","ammo":10,"concealed":false}],
      "persistent": {"tacNetRelay": true},
      "netPenalty": 0
    }
  },
  "map": {"keyTerrain": ["cities","pipelines","ports"], "losRules": "range-3 blocked by mtn/urban"}
}
```

### Resolution and Dice

- Use d20 with modifiers; success on roll ≥ Target Number (TN)
  - Long‑range fires TN = 12; apply modifiers: concealment (−2), cyber/EW (−4 or −7), counter‑battery present (−2), ISR quality (+1 to +3)
- Intercepts: defender rolls d20 vs TN 12 with cyber/EW penalties applied to IADS
- ASW/SSN: detection roll before attack; on success resolve naval strike (TN 11)

### Cyber/EW Model (Theatre Level)

- BLUE/RED may pool AP to launch B‑SOD‑style packages:
  - Minor Disruption: −2 to enemy long‑range fires and intercepts this turn (cost 2 AP)
  - Major Disruption: −4 persistent for 2 turns unless repaired (cost 4 AP + repairable)
  - Severe Disruption: −7 this turn, degrades to −4 next; repairable (cost 6 AP)
- Repair: spend 2 AP to reduce penalty by 2 (cannot exceed 0)

### Influence Meter and Info Ops

- Track from −10 (Red dominant) to +10 (Blue dominant)
- Shifts per turn: casualties (±1 to ±3), territorial change (±1), humanitarian ops (+1), atrocities/deepfakes (±1 to ±3), naval blockade progress (±1)
- Thresholds: +6/−6 confer international support penalties/bonuses (e.g., sanctions, logistics modifiers)

### Fog‑of‑War and Visibility

- Units have `concealed: true/false`; visibility requires LOS and ISR success
- Successful ISR marks targets for fires (+2 to TN for one attack); jamming reduces ISR success by −2

### Event Deck (Curveballs)

- Space Debris Surge: set `spaceDenied = true` this turn; all GNSS‑reliant actions −2
- UAS Swarm: increase ISR success by +2 for the advantaged side this turn
- Comm Outage (Local): one sector suffers −2 to C2; ground moves limited
- Civilian Outcry: influence shifts −2 towards side causing collateral damage

### Victory Conditions (Examples)

- BLUE: maintain control of all cities and ports for 6 turns; resource fields ≥ 80% held; influence ≥ +2
- RED: seize 2 resource fields and cut southern sea access for 2 consecutive turns; influence ≤ −2

### CLI Prompt Templates (for LLM)

- System: “You are the UMPIRE. Enforce the rules, roll dice, apply fog‑of‑war. Keep secret state hidden unless revealed.”
- User (BLUE): “Turn 3. AP=8. Objectives: defend cities, preserve forces. Orders: [JSON with actions per domain].”
- Assistant (UMPIRE): “Acknowledged. Resolving ISR… [dice + outcomes]. Cyber/EW… Air/Maritime… Fires… Ground… Info Ops… Logistics. Public state: {…}. Hidden notes recorded.”

### Notes for Balance and Playtesting

- Start with flat modifiers (−4/−7) to test CDC sequencing, then add nuance (system‑specific degradation, diminishing returns)
- Increase small‑UAS density; lower fog‑of‑war near front to speed targeting cycles
- Consider a forced `spaceDenied` turn to test resilience without space services
